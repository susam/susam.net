Growing Up With the Long Scale

While growing up during my childhood days, as a result of stumbling upon a very old dictionary lying around in our house, long scale names for large numbers involving powers of thousand. For example, the dictionary taught me that a million is \( 10^6 \), a billion is \( 10^{12} \), a trillion is \( 10^{18} \), a quadrillion is \( 10^{24} \), and so on.

The long scale names made a lot of sense to me. It was quite intuitive to me. If I multiply a million with itself twice, I get a billion, i.e., \( (10^6)^2 = 10^{12} \). Similarly, if I multiply a million with itself three times, I get a trillion (\( (10^6)^3 = 10^{18} \)). Similarly, if I multiply a million with itself four times, I get a quadrillion (\( (10^6)^4 = 10^{24} \)). In other words, a million million is a billion, a million million million is a trillion, and so on. It was simple and straightforward.

Imagine my disappointment, as I left home to enter university, got access to computers and the World Wide Web and realised that the names I learnt growing up were off by several orders of magnitude compared to the popular convention used in most of the English-speaking world and most certainly in the computing world. The long scale names I learnt as a child were irrelevant in the new world I was entering. It was the short scale names that had won the computing and technology world. In the short scale, a billion was no longer a million million. Instead, it was only a thousand million! Similarly, a trillion was no longer a million million million. Instead, it was only a million million! I couldn't see the logic or the sense in these names! How in the world can a million million be a trillion? Where does the "tri" even come from when I have only multiplied a million twice? As we take bigger numbers, the short scale names begin to make even less sense. A million million million million is not a quadrillion as I was so familiar with in the long scale. In the short scale, a million million million million is a septillion? What the heck? What is going on!

Naturally, I had to go through a period of adjustment to give up my intuition for the long scale names and get accustomed to the short scale names. At the same time, I could also trick my mind into making sense of the short scale names. As I mentioned before, the long names were quite intuitive to understand. They are determined by how many times we multiply a million with itself. For example, a million times million times million is a trillion. Similarly, a million multiplied by itself five times is a quintillion. How can we make sense of the short scale names?

Making Sense of the Short Scale Names

We have seen before that the long scale names are determined by how many times we multiply a million with itself. In other words, they are determined by the \( n \) in the number \( 10^{6n} \). For example, if \( n = 5 \), then we get the number \( 10^{30} \) and we call it a quintillion. However, in the short scale, we call the same number a nonillion! How exactly can we make sense of it?

Here is one way I made sense of the short scale names when I first encountered them in my university days. It is how many additional thousands we add as multipliers to one thousand that determines the names. If we multiple a thousand with one more thousand, we get a million. But if we add a thousand with a thousand and then by one more thousand, we get a billion. Similarly, if we add a thousand with a thousand, then by one more thousand, and then by one more thousand, we get a trillion. And so on! It is not quite as neat as the long scale names. But it works.

Yet another way to describe this is to say that the short scale names are determined by the \( n \) in the number \( 1000 \times 1000^n \). If \( n = 1 \), we get \( 10^6 \) which we call a million. If \( n = 9 \), we get \( 10^{30} \) which we call a nonillion! More succinctly, it is the \( n \) in the number \( 10^{3(n + 1)} \) that determines the name of the number.
